In addition, many applications require a valid passport and, in some cases, proof of English proficiency such as IELTS or TOEFL. While these requirements may seem standard, what differentiates successful applicants is the quality and readiness of these documents. For example, recommendation letters should be strong and personalised, not generic, and your CV should clearly highlight achievements, leadership, and impact rather than just responsibilities. The key is to prepare a “master file” with all documents updated and ready to adapt for each application. This reduces stress, saves time, and allows you to focus on tailoring your application for each opportunity.

### **Q: How do I write a strong motivation letter?**

**A:** A strong motivation letter is not about telling your life story—it is about connecting your past, present, and future clearly and compellingly. Begin by explaining what sparked your interest in the field (BIG WHY), then show what you have done to develop that interest through studies, projects, or experiences. The most important part is demonstrating impact—what problem (BIG WHY) you care about and how your work or ideas contribute to solving it. Avoid generic statements and focus on specificity, such as particular experiences, achievements, or goals. Then clearly explain why this scholarship or program is the right fit for you and how it aligns with your long-term vision. A good motivation letter feels intentional, focused, and authentic. It should answer one key question: why you, why this program, and why now. When done well, it becomes your strongest tool for standing out.

### **Q: How important are grades?**

**A:** Grades are important, but they are not the only factor in scholarship selection. Most competitive scholarships look for a balance between academic performance, leadership potential, community service, awards/honours, Scientific Publications, conference presentations and demonstrated impact. Strong grades show discipline and intellectual ability, but they do not tell the full story of who you are or what you can achieve. Many successful applicants complement good academic performance with extracurricular involvement, community engagement, or professional experience. If your grades are not perfect, you can still strengthen your application by showing growth, consistency, and commitment in other areas. What matters most is how well you present your overall profile. Think of grades as a foundation, not the entire structure. A well-rounded candidate who shows initiative, purpose, and direction often stands out more than someone with perfect grades but no clear sense of impact or vision.

### **Q: Do I need leadership experience?**

**A:** Leadership experience is highly valued in most scholarship applications, but it is often misunderstood. Leadership does not mean holding a formal title or position; ***it means taking initiative, solving problems, and influencing outcomes in any setting.*** For example, organising a student activity, leading a group project, mentoring peers, or starting a small initiative in your community all count as leadership. Scholarship committees are interested in your ability to create impact, not just your title. What matters is how you describe your role—what challenge you faced, what action you took, and what result you achieved. Even small experiences can demonstrate strong leadership if they show responsibility and initiative. The key is to reflect on your experiences and present them clearly. Leadership is less about authority and more about action and influence.

### **Q: Can I apply without work experience?**

**A:** Yes, many scholarships accept applicants without formal work experience, especially for undergraduate and some master’s programs. However, even if you lack professional experience, you should still demonstrate engagement through internships, volunteer work, academic projects, or extracurricular activities. These experiences show that you are proactive and willing to apply what you learn in real-world contexts. Some scholarships, such as Chevening, do require a minimum amount of work experience, but others focus more on academic potential and leadership qualities. The key is to present whatever experience you have in a meaningful way, highlighting skills, contributions, and learning outcomes. Work experience is just one form of demonstrating readiness; what matters most is showing initiative, growth, and a clear sense of direction.

### **Q: How many scholarships should I apply for?**

**A:** You should approach scholarship applications as a strategic process rather than relying on a single opportunity. Ideally, aim to apply for at least five to ten scholarships, depending on your eligibility. This increases your chances of success and allows you to refine your application skills over time. Each application improves your ability to write stronger essays, present your achievements, and respond to questions effectively. It is important to balance quantity with quality, ensuring that each application is well-prepared and tailored to the specific program. Many successful candidates do not win on their first attempt—they improve through multiple applications. Consistency and persistence are key. Treat the process as a long-term investment rather than a one-time effort.

### **Q: What is a CV for scholarships?**

**A:** A scholarship CV is different from a job CV because it focuses more on academic achievements, leadership, and impact rather than purely professional experience. It should clearly present your education, relevant coursework, research, projects, leadership roles, volunteer work, and any awards or recognitions. Instead of listing responsibilities, focus on achievements and contributions—what you did, how you did it, and what changed as a result. Keep it concise, well-structured, and easy to read. Use clear headings and bullet points to organise information. A strong CV tells a story of growth, initiative, and direction. It should reflect not just what you have done, but who you are becoming.

### **Q: How do I prepare for interviews?**

**A:** Preparing for scholarship interviews requires clarity, reflection, and practice. Start by understanding your own story—your background, interests, achievements, and goals. Be ready to explain why you chose your field, what impact you want to create, and why the scholarship is important to your journey. Practice answering common questions, but avoid memorising responses. Instead, focus on understanding your key points so you can speak naturally and confidently. It also helps to research the scholarship and align your answers with its values and objectives. Mock interviews with friends or mentors can help you improve your delivery and confidence. The goal is not to sound perfect, but to be clear, authentic, and thoughtful in your responses.

**Q: Can I win without connections?**

**A:** Yes, most scholarships are designed to be merit-based and accessible to all qualified applicants, regardless of personal connections. While networking can provide information and guidance, it is not a requirement for success. What matters most is the strength of your application—your academic record, experiences, motivation, and clarity of purpose. Many successful candidates come from backgrounds with limited access to networks but succeed through preparation, persistence, and strategic application. Focus on what you can control: building a strong profile, preparing quality documents, and applying consistently. Connections may open doors, but preparation ensures you can walk through them.

### **Q: What fields are most funded?**

**A:** Scholarships are often aligned with global development priorities, which means certain fields receive more funding than others. Areas such as health sciences, environmental studies, engineering, technology, and development-related fields are commonly supported because they address critical global challenges. Environmental Health Science, for example, is highly relevant due to its connection to public health, sustainability, and climate issues. However, this does not mean other fields are excluded. Funding exists across disciplines, but the key is how well you align your interests with broader societal needs. When you clearly demonstrate the relevance and impact of your field, your chances of securing funding increase significantly.

### **Q: How early should I start?**

**A:** The best time to start preparing for scholarships is at least one year before the application deadlines. This allows enough time to gather documents, improve your CV, gain relevant experience, and prepare strong applications. Early preparation also allows you to identify suitable scholarships and understand their requirements. Many students start too late and end up rushing their applications, which reduces their chances of success. Starting early allows you to work strategically and confidently. It transforms the process from stressful to manageable.

### **Q: What mistakes should I avoid?**

**A:** Common mistakes in scholarship applications include applying late, submitting generic essays, and failing to research the scholarship properly. Many applicants reuse the same motivation letter for different programs without tailoring it, which reduces its impact. Others overlook small details such as formatting, document requirements, or deadlines. Another major mistake is underestimating the importance of clarity and structure in writing. Avoid rushing your application and take time to review and refine your documents. Paying attention to detail can make a significant difference.

### **Q: How do I stand out?**

**A:** Standing out in a competitive pool requires clarity, consistency, and evidence of impact. Instead of trying to impress with everything you have done, focus on presenting a clear direction and purpose. Show how your experiences connect to your goals and how you have made a difference, even in small ways. Consistency across your CV, motivation letter, and interview responses is key. Scholarship committees are looking for candidates who are not only capable but also intentional and driven. When your story is clear, and your actions support it, you naturally stand out.

### **Q: Is IELTS/TOEFL required?**

**A:** In many cases, English proficiency tests such as IELTS or TOEFL are required, especially for programs in English-speaking countries. However, some universities and scholarships waive this requirement if your previous education was conducted in English. It is important to check the specific requirements of each program. If the test is required, plan to take it early so you have enough time to retake it if needed. Preparing well for the test can improve your chances of meeting the required scores.

### **Q: How do I stay consistent?**

**A:** Consistency is one of the most important factors in successfully securing scholarships. Treat the application process like a structured project with clear goals and timelines. Set weekly targets, such as completing one section of an application or researching new opportunities. Track your progress and hold yourself accountable. It also helps to create a routine and dedicate specific time to applications. Motivation may fluctuate, but systems keep you moving forward. Over time, small consistent efforts lead to significant results.
